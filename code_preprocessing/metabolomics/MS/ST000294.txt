#PROJECT
PR:PROJECT_TITLE                 	Mechanisms of Metabolic Cycles in Diapausing Flesh Fly by Metabolomics Approach
PR:PROJECT_TYPE                  	Time course
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	Insects use diapause, a programmed period of dormancy, to avoid stressful times
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	of the year and to exploit seasonal times of resource availability. Because most
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	diapausing insects do not feed, they must live off their body reserves for
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	several months and the proper use of metabolic reserves is critical for
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	surviving diapause and performing after diapause termination. Across multiple
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	insects, metabolic depression during diapause has been associated with a switch
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	from aerobic metabolism to facultative anaerobic metabolism, despite insects not
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	suffering environmental oxygen limitation. While metabolic rates are depressed
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	during diapause overall to save energy, some insects show regular cyclical bouts
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	of higher metabolic activity during diapause. The functional importance of these
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	metabolic cycles and the mechanisms underlying these cycles are still unknown,
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	but they may be critical for properly maintaining the balance between energy
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	states and purge the accumulation of anaerobic metabolic byproducts. In the
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	present study, we will test the hypothesis that periodic cycles of increased
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	metabolism during insect diapause are associated with both regenerating
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	organismal energetic states, particularly ATP that may decline during metabolic
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	depression, and for purging metabolites associated with anaerobic metabolism. We
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	will use a combination of non-targeted uHPLC-MS/MS metabolomics and targeted
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	NMR-spectroscopy to identify and quantify metabolites that are altered during
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	the cycles in diapausing pupae of the flesh fly, Sarcophaga crassipalpis. This
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	work will allow us to propose specific biochemical and cellular hypotheses for
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	the regulation of cyclic releases from metabolic depression in diapausing
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	insects. Our work may not only reveal the physiological mechanisms regulating
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	metabolic cycles during diapause in flesh fly, but also provide insight to
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	understand the regulation of similar metabolic cycles in mammalian hibernators
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	(i.e., periodic arousal), and also provide insights into how these cycles could
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	be exploited to disrupt the diapause of insect pests.
