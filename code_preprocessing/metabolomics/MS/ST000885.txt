#PROJECT
PR:PROJECT_TITLE                 	Gut Microbiota Modulate Brain Insulin Sensitivity and Neurobehavior
PR:PROJECT_TYPE                  	Metabolite profiling of cecal contents and brains of mice under diet-induced
PR:PROJECT_TYPE                  	obesity (DIO) with and without antibiotic treatments.
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	Obesity and diabetes in humans are associated with increased rates of anxiety
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	and depression. To understand the role of the gut microbiome and brain insulin
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	resistance in these disorders, we evaluated behaviors and insulin action in
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	brain of mice with diet-induced obesity (DIO) with and without antibiotic
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	treatment. We find that DIO mice have behaviors reflective of increased anxiety
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	and depression. This is associated with decreased insulin signaling and
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	increased inflammation in multiple brain regions. Treatment with oral
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	metronidazole or vancomycin decreases inflammation, improves insulin signaling
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	in the brain and reduces signs of anxiety and depression. These effects are
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	transferable to germ-free mice by fecal transplant of gut microbiota and are
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	associated with changes in the levels of tryptophan, GABA, BDNF, amino acids and
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	multiple acylcarnitines in the brain. Thus, changes in gut microbiota can
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	control brain insulin signaling and metabolite levels, and this leads to altered
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	neurobehaviors.
