PR:PROJECT_TITLE                 	Plasma Metabolomic Profiles Reflective of Glucose Homeostasis in Non-Diabetic
PR:PROJECT_TITLE                 	and Type 2 Diabetic Obese African-American Women
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	Insulin resistance progressing to type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) is marked by a
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	broad perturbation of macronutrient intermediary metabolism. Understanding the
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	biochemical networks that underlie metabolic homeostasis and how they associate
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	with insulin action will help unravel diabetes etiology and should foster
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	discovery of new biomarkers of disease risk and severity. We examined
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	differences in plasma concentrations of >350 metabolites in fasted obese T2DM
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	vs. obese non-diabetic African-American women, and utilized principal components
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	analysis to identify 158 metabolite components that strongly correlated with
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	fasting HbA1c over a broad range of the latter (r?=??0.631; p<0.0001). In
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	addition to many unidentified small molecules, specific metabolites that were
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	increased significantly in T2DM subjects included certain amino acids and their
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	derivatives (i.e., leucine, 2-ketoisocaproate, valine, cystine, histidine),
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	2-hydroxybutanoate, long-chain fatty acids, and carbohydrate derivatives.
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	Leucine and valine concentrations rose with increasing HbA1c, and significantly
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	correlated with plasma acetylcarnitine concentrations. It is hypothesized that
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	this reflects a close link between abnormalities in glucose homeostasis, amino
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	acid catabolism, and efficiency of fuel combustion in the tricarboxylic acid
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	(TCA) cycle. It is speculated that a mechanism for potential TCA cycle
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	inefficiency concurrent with insulin resistance is “anaplerotic stress”
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	emanating from reduced amino acid-derived carbon flux to TCA cycle
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	intermediates, which if coupled to perturbation in cataplerosis would lead to
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	net reduction in TCA cycle capacity relative to fuel delivery.
