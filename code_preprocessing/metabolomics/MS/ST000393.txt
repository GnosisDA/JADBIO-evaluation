#PROJECT
PR:PROJECT_TITLE                 	Long-Chain Fatty Acid Combustion Rate Is Associated with Unique Metabolite
PR:PROJECT_TITLE                 	Profiles in Skeletal Muscle Mitochondria
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	Incomplete or limited long-chain fatty acid (LCFA) combustion in skeletal muscle
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	has been associated with insulin resistance. Signals that are responsive to
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	shifts in LCFA β-oxidation rate or degree of intramitochondrial catabolism are
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	hypothesized to regulate second messenger systems downstream of the insulin
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	receptor. Recent evidence supports a causal link between mitochondrial LCFA
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	combustion in skeletal muscle and insulin resistance. We have used unbiased
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	metabolite profiling of mouse muscle mitochondria with the aim of identifying
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	candidate metabolites within or effluxed from mitochondria and that are shifted
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	with LCFA combustion rate. This proof-of-principle study establishes that
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	large-scale metabolomics methods can be applied to organelle-level models to
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	discover metabolite patterns reflective of LCFA combustion, which may lead to
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	identification of molecules linking muscle fat metabolism and insulin signaling.
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	Our results suggest that future studies should focus on the fate of effluxed TCA
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	cycle intermediates and on mechanisms ensuring their replenishment during LCFA
PR:PROJECT_SUMMARY               	metabolism in skeletal muscle.

#COLLECTION
CO:COLLECTION_SUMMARY            	Skeletal muscle mitochondria were isolated essentially according to Chappell and
CO:COLLECTION_SUMMARY            	Perry [Chappell JB, Perry SV (1954) Biochemical and osmotic properties of
CO:COLLECTION_SUMMARY            	skeletal muscle mitochondria. Nature 173: 1094–1095.]. All media were
CO:COLLECTION_SUMMARY            	ice-cold, and procedures done on ice or at 4°C. Briefly, pectoral, forelimb and
CO:COLLECTION_SUMMARY            	hindlimb muscles were rapidly dissected and placed in basic medium [BM (mM): KCl
CO:COLLECTION_SUMMARY            	(140), HEPES (20), MgCl2 (5), EGTA (2); pH 7.0]. Together, these muscle groups
CO:COLLECTION_SUMMARY            	comprise a mixed population of mainly type II oxidative and glycolytic fibers.
CO:COLLECTION_SUMMARY            	Muscle was cleaned of connective tissue and fat, minced and placed in 15 vol of
CO:COLLECTION_SUMMARY            	homogenizing medium (HM: BM with 1 mM ATP and 1% BSA (w/v)) containing one unit
CO:COLLECTION_SUMMARY            	of protease (Subtilisin A) per g muscle wet weight.

SP:SAMPLEPREP_SUMMARY            	rpm) and fractionated by centrifugation at 800 g (10 min), and the supernatant
SP:SAMPLEPREP_SUMMARY            	collected and spun at 12000 g (9 min). The pellet was resuspended in 20 ml BM
SP:SAMPLEPREP_SUMMARY            	and incubated on ice for 5 min (myofibrillar repolymerization). Samples were
SP:SAMPLEPREP_SUMMARY            	spun at 800 g (8 min) to pellet actin-myosin polymers. The supernatant was then
SP:SAMPLEPREP_SUMMARY            	spun at 12000 g (9 min). The final pellet was resuspended in 220 µl of BM.
